## When do triggered abilities trigger? [#triggered-ability-timing]

When their condition is met, triggered abilities are added to the chain as pending items, regardless of whose turn it is.<Rule number="383.3" /><Rule number="383.3.c" />

The <Reaction /> keyword is not required for this to work on an opponent's turn.
Unlike spells and activated abilities which require <Reaction /> to be played outside your turn, satisfying a trigger condition automatically creates the pending ability.
That ability must still finalize: it may be removed if its controller declines an initial "you may," if legal choices are unavailable, or if a required trigger cost is not paid.<Rule number="383.3.a.2" /><Rule number="402.4" /><Rule number="404.2" />

<Callout type="idea" title="Example">
	<Card name="Blade Dancer" /> triggers when you choose a friendly unit.
	This can happen on your opponent's turn, for example when you play a <Reaction /> spell that targets one of your own units.
</Callout>

## Can I react to triggered and activated abilities? [#ability-reactions]

Yes, with one important exception: abilities that use the <Add /> keyword.

All triggered and activated abilities are placed on the chain.<Rule number="377.3" /><Rule number="383.3" />
However, abilities that add resources resolve immediately when finalized, which means players do not receive priority to react to them.<Rule number="337.2" /><Rule number="429.2" /><Rule number="429.2.a" />

## Does removing the source of a triggered ability stop it? [#source-removal]

Usually no — once a triggered ability has finalized, removing its source does not remove or counter the ability on the chain.<Rule number="383.3" /><Rule number="406.2" />
The ability still resolves and executes its instructions like a spell.<Rule number="406.5" />
To stop the chain item itself, an effect must specifically instruct a player to counter it.<Rule number="425.2.a" /><Rule number="425.3" />

However, removing the source can prevent an instruction from working if it needs information from that source.
Source referents such as "here," "my," or "this battlefield" are checked when the instruction executes.<Rule number="359.3.f.1" /><Rule number="359.3.f.2" />
If the source is no longer on the board, information such as its location is unavailable, and an instruction that needs that information is ignored.<Rule number="359.3.e.6" /><Rule number="359.3.e.12" />

<Callout type="idea" title="Examples">
	If an opponent responds to <Card name="Ashe, Focused" />'s play effect with <Card name="Star-Crossed" /> and returns Ashe to her owner's hand, her ability still resolves.
	Nothing in its instructions requires Ashe to remain on the board.

	If Star-Crossed returns <Card name="Vi, Peacekeeper" /> before her attack trigger resolves, Vi cannot <Stun /> an enemy unit "here."
	Because Vi is no longer on the board, she has no location for "here" to reference, so that instruction is ignored.

	The same applies to <Card name="Azir, Sovereign" />: if Azir leaves the board before his attack trigger resolves, there is no "this battlefield" to which his token units can move.
</Callout>

## How are additional conditions in triggered abilities evaluated? [#trigger-time-conditions]

It depends on where the condition appears in the ability's text.

The trigger condition begins with "When," "At," or "The [Nth] time" and includes the event and any qualifiers attached to it.<Rule number="383.2.a" />
An additional conditional statement immediately following that condition is also part of the trigger condition, even if it appears after a comma.<Rule number="383.2.a.1" />
The remaining instructions form the ability's effect.<Rule number="383.2.b" />

Every requirement in the trigger condition must be satisfied for the ability to be placed on the chain.
For example, <Card name="Sunken Temple" /> reads "When you conquer here with one or more <Mighty /> units, you may pay <Energy value={1} /> to draw 1."
The requirement that the conquering units be <Mighty /> is embedded in the trigger condition; if no <Mighty /> unit participated in the conquer, the ability does not trigger.

An "if" clause that immediately follows the trigger condition is therefore part of that condition and checked when the triggering event occurs.
If the condition is not satisfied at the moment the triggering event occurs, the ability is never placed on the chain at all.<Rule number="383.2.a.1" />

However, "if" clauses embedded within the effect text — coming after an effect instruction rather than immediately following the trigger condition — are only checked on resolution.
The ability goes on the chain regardless, and the condition is only evaluated when it resolves.

<Callout type="idea" title="Example">
	<Card name="Gutter Palace" /> reads "At the start of your Beginning Phase, if you have exactly 4 cards in hand and exactly 4 units at battlefields, you win the game."

	The "if" clause immediately follows the trigger condition, so it is part of that condition and checked when the triggering event occurs.
	This ability is only placed on the chain if you already have exactly 4 cards in hand and exactly 4 units at battlefields at the start of your Beginning Phase.
	If the condition is not satisfied at that moment, the ability does not trigger at all.
</Callout>

<Callout type="idea" title="Example">
	<Card name="Loose Cannon" /> reads "At start of your Beginning Phase, draw 1 if you have one or fewer cards in your hand."

	Here the "if" clause does not immediately follow the trigger condition — it comes after the effect instruction "draw 1".
	This means it is part of the effect and is checked on resolution.
	The ability goes on the chain at the start of every one of your Beginning Phases, regardless of how many cards are in your hand.
	Only when the ability resolves is the hand size checked; if you have more than one card at that point, the draw does not happen.
</Callout>

## When do I decide whether to perform a triggered ability that says "you may"? [#optional-trigger-decision]

It depends on where "you may" appears in the ability's effect.

If "you may" is the first part of the effect, you decide whether to perform the ability during finalization.<Rule number="383.3.a" /><Rule number="402.1" />
If you decline, the pending ability is removed from the chain and considered not to have triggered.<Rule number="383.3.a.2" /><Rule number="402.1.a" />
If you accept, you make all choices required for finalization and pay any costs incurred during finalization before the ability can finalize.<Rule number="402.2" /><Rule number="404" />

If "you may" appears later in the effect, you decide whether to perform that instruction when the ability resolves.<Rule number="383.3.a.3" />

## When do I pay costs within instructions using "may"? [#costs-within-instructions]

It depends on where the cost appears in the triggered ability's effect.

In rules text phrased as "[do X] to [do Y]," the [do X] part is considered a cost within instructions.
If that cost appears at the beginning of a triggered effect or immediately after an initial "you may," it becomes the ability's base cost and must be paid during finalization.<Rule number="383.3.b" /><Rule number="383.3.b.1" /><Rule number="740.4.a.2" /><Rule number="403.1.b.1" />
If you decline to pay, the pending ability is removed from the chain without finalizing.<Rule number="404.2" />

If the cost appears later in the triggered effect, you pay it when the ability resolves.<Rule number="740.4.a.2.a" />

<Callout type="idea" title="Example">
	<Card name="Vayne, Hunter" />'s ability reads "When I conquer, you may pay <Energy value={1} /> to return me to my owner's hand."
	It will trigger when you conquer.
	At finalization, you must decide whether to pay the <Energy value={1} /> cost.
	If you decline, the pending ability is removed from the chain.
	If you pay, the ability finalizes and opponents have the opportunity to react before it resolves.
</Callout>
